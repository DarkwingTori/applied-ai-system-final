# Pet Nutrition Guidelines

## Dogs

### Feeding Frequency
- Puppies (under 6 months): 3–4 meals per day
- Adolescent dogs (6–12 months): 2–3 meals per day
- Adult dogs (1–7 years): 2 meals per day (morning and evening) is optimal. Once-daily feeding can cause bloat risk in large breeds.
- Senior dogs (7+ years): 2–3 smaller meals per day to ease digestion.

### Portion Guidelines
A general starting point is 2–3% of body weight in food per day, adjusted for activity level and food caloric density. Always follow the feeding guidelines on your specific food bag and adjust based on body condition score (you should feel ribs but not see them).

### Choosing a Food
- Look for an AAFCO statement indicating "complete and balanced" for the appropriate life stage.
- The first ingredient should be a named animal protein (chicken, beef, salmon — not "meat by-products" as the primary ingredient).
- Avoid foods with artificial colors, BHA, BHT, or ethoxyquin as preservatives.
- Grain-free diets have been linked to dilated cardiomyopathy in some dogs; consult your vet before choosing one.

### Foods Toxic to Dogs
- Grapes and raisins (kidney failure)
- Chocolate (theobromine toxicity)
- Xylitol (found in sugar-free products — causes rapid blood sugar drop)
- Onions and garlic (damage red blood cells)
- Macadamia nuts (neurological effects)
- Avocado (persin toxicity, especially in birds and rabbits; can cause GI upset in dogs)
- Alcohol and caffeine
- Cooked bones (splinter and cause internal injury)

## Cats

### Feeding Frequency
- Kittens (under 6 months): 3–4 meals per day
- Adult cats (1–7 years): 2 meals per day; some do well with measured free-feeding if not prone to overeating.
- Senior cats (7+): 2–3 meals per day; wet food is recommended to increase moisture intake.

### Protein Requirements
Cats are obligate carnivores — they require animal-based protein and cannot synthesize taurine or arachidonic acid on their own. A high-protein, moderate-fat, low-carbohydrate diet best matches their natural biology.

### Hydration
Cats have a low thirst drive and often become mildly dehydrated on dry-food-only diets. Incorporating wet food (at least 50% of diet) dramatically improves hydration. Water fountains encourage cats to drink more.

### Foods Toxic to Cats
- Onions and garlic (Heinz body anemia)
- Grapes and raisins
- Alcohol and caffeine
- Xylitol
- Raw dough (yeast expands in stomach)
- Dairy (most adult cats are lactose intolerant)
- Chocolate

## Treats
Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily caloric intake for both dogs and cats. Use treats for training and enrichment, not as a main food source.

## Weight Management
- Overweight pets have significantly shorter lifespans and higher rates of diabetes, arthritis, and heart disease.
- Use a measuring cup for every meal — "eyeballing" is the leading cause of overfeeding.
- If your pet needs to lose weight, reduce food by 10–15% and recheck in 2–4 weeks; avoid crash diets.
- Puzzle feeders and food toys slow eating and increase mental engagement.
