# Pet Enrichment Guidelines

## What Is Enrichment?
Enrichment refers to activities and environmental features that stimulate a pet's natural behaviors — sniffing, chasing, foraging, problem-solving — to promote mental and physical wellbeing. Boredom is a leading cause of destructive behavior and stress in pets.

## Dog Enrichment

### Nose Work and Scent Games
- Dogs experience the world primarily through smell; sniffing is mentally exhausting in the best way.
- Hide treats around the house or yard for "find it" games.
- Snuffle mats (fabric mats with fabric strips to hide kibble in) slow feeding and engage the nose.
- Formal nose work and scent detection classes are available for dogs who love this activity.

### Puzzle Feeders
- Slow feeders, Kongs, and puzzle bowls make dogs work for their food.
- Freeze a Kong stuffed with peanut butter, banana, or wet food for a longer-lasting activity.
- Rotate between different puzzle feeders to maintain novelty.

### Training as Enrichment
- 10–15 minutes of training per day provides significant mental stimulation.
- Teaching new tricks keeps aging dogs mentally sharp.
- Trick training, agility, and obedience work strengthen the human-pet bond.

### Social Enrichment
- Regular playdates with known, compatible dogs.
- Dog parks (for appropriate dogs) provide socialization and off-leash running.
- Car rides and new environments provide novel stimulation.

### Chewing
- Chewing is a natural stress reliever for dogs.
- Provide appropriate chews: bully sticks, raw bones (not cooked), antlers, or rope toys.
- Always supervise chewing and discard broken pieces that could be swallowed.

## Cat Enrichment

### Hunting Simulation
- Wand toys that mimic prey movement (erratic, unpredictable) are highly engaging.
- End play sessions with a "kill" — letting the cat catch and bite the toy — followed by a small treat to complete the hunt-eat cycle.
- Automated laser toys and remote-controlled mice provide stimulation when you're busy.

### Foraging
- Instead of food bowls, scatter feed kibble across a mat or use puzzle feeders.
- Hide small treats in cardboard boxes and paper bags.
- Licki mats with wet food, peanut butter (xylitol-free), or baby food provide licking enrichment.

### Environmental Enrichment
- Cat trees of different heights allow climbing and perching.
- Window bird feeders create "cat TV."
- Catios (outdoor enclosures) safely provide fresh air and outdoor stimulation.
- Rotate toys regularly; new toys every 1–2 weeks re-engage interest.

### Social and Cognitive Enrichment
- Most cats enjoy brief, focused training sessions with high-value treats; they can learn sit, high-five, and other tricks.
- Paper bags, tunnels, and cardboard boxes are low-cost entertainment.
- Some cats enjoy watching cat-specific videos (birds, fish) on a tablet or TV.

## Signs of Under-Enrichment
- Excessive grooming or repetitive behaviors (pacing, tail chasing)
- Aggression toward people or other pets without clear trigger
- Destructive behavior (scratching furniture, chewing objects)
- Attention-seeking behaviors (constant vocalizing, knocking things off shelves)
- Withdrawal, lethargy, or depression
