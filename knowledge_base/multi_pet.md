# Multi-Pet Household Guidelines

## Introducing New Pets

### Dog to Dog Introduction
- Introduce dogs on neutral territory (a park, not your home) so neither dog feels territorial.
- Keep both dogs on loose leashes; tight leashes communicate tension.
- Allow brief sniffing, then redirect to walking side by side before going home.
- Separate dogs initially at home using baby gates; allow visual access before full contact.
- Feed separately for the first few weeks to prevent resource guarding.

### Cat to Cat Introduction
- Keep the new cat in a separate room with their own food, water, and litter box for 1–2 weeks.
- Exchange bedding between cats so they can smell each other.
- Introduce through a cracked door before face-to-face meetings.
- Expect some hissing and swatting — this is normal. Intervene only if there is injury.
- Integration typically takes 2–4 weeks; some cats take 2–3 months.

### Dog and Cat Introduction
- Keep the dog on a leash during initial meetings.
- Allow the cat to approach on their own terms; never force contact.
- Ensure the cat always has elevated escape routes (cat trees, counters) the dog cannot access.
- Separate them when unsupervised until you are fully confident in their interactions.

## Scheduling for Multiple Pets
When managing multiple pets, stagger walk times if dogs have very different exercise needs. Group tasks when possible (feeding multiple pets at the same time) to streamline routines. Assign each pet their own space for eating to prevent resource guarding.

## Resource Management
The golden rules for multi-pet households:
- One litter box per cat plus one extra
- One water bowl per pet, in multiple locations
- One bed per pet (even if they share — give each their own option)
- Feed separately or supervise all mealtimes to prevent food aggression
- Multiple toys so pets don't compete

## Managing Conflict
Signs of unhealthy dynamics:
- One pet consistently blocking another from food, water, or exits
- Injuries from fights
- One pet hiding and refusing to come out
- Loss of appetite or inappropriate elimination due to stress

Consult a veterinary behaviorist if conflicts do not improve with management. Medication may be appropriate for highly anxious pets.

## Time and Attention
With multiple pets, ensure each animal receives individual attention daily. Group playtime is great but does not replace one-on-one bonding time, especially for dogs and cats who may feel overlooked in a busy household.

## Emergency Planning
Have separate carriers and identification for each pet. In an emergency evacuation, you need a plan for transporting all animals. Keep vaccination records for each pet in one accessible location.
