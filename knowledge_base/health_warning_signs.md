# Pet Health Warning Signs

## When to Call Your Vet Immediately (Emergency)

### Dogs and Cats
- Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing in cats, or blue/grey gums
- Collapse or inability to stand
- Seizures (especially first-time or lasting more than 2–3 minutes)
- Suspected ingestion of toxins (chocolate, xylitol, medications, household chemicals)
- Bloated or distended abdomen, especially with unproductive retching in large dogs (bloat/GDV is life-threatening)
- Eye injuries or sudden vision loss
- Urinary blockage: straining to urinate with little or no output — especially critical in male cats (can be fatal within 24–48 hours)
- Profuse or uncontrolled bleeding
- Suspected broken bones
- Loss of consciousness

## Warning Signs to Address Within 24 Hours

- Vomiting more than 2–3 times in a day
- Diarrhea with blood, or lasting more than 24 hours
- Refusal to eat for more than 24 hours (cats: over 12–18 hours can trigger hepatic lipidosis)
- Significant lethargy — pet cannot be roused with normal enthusiasm
- Limping that doesn't improve with rest
- Swollen or red eyes, or discharge from eyes
- Head shaking or scratching at ears
- Unusual lump or swelling that appeared suddenly

## Monitoring at Home

### Vital Signs (Normal Ranges)
- **Dogs**: Heart rate 60–140 bpm; Respiration 15–30 breaths/min; Temperature 99.5–102.5°F
- **Cats**: Heart rate 140–220 bpm; Respiration 20–30 breaths/min; Temperature 100–102.5°F

### Weight Monitoring
Weigh pets monthly. Unexplained weight loss of more than 10% of body weight warrants a vet visit. Weight loss in cats can indicate hyperthyroidism, diabetes, kidney disease, or cancer.

### Dental Health Check
Lift your pet's lips monthly and look for:
- Yellow or brown tartar buildup
- Red, swollen, or bleeding gums
- Loose or broken teeth
- Bad breath (beyond normal "dog breath")

### Coat and Skin Check
Run your hands over your pet's body weekly to feel for:
- New lumps or bumps
- Hair loss or thinning patches
- Redness, scaling, or excessive dandruff
- Cuts, sores, or puncture wounds

## Preventive Care Schedule
- **Annual vet exam**: All adult pets
- **Senior pets**: Every 6 months
- **Vaccinations**: Follow your vet's protocol (varies by region and lifestyle)
- **Flea/tick/heartworm prevention**: Monthly, year-round
- **Dental cleaning**: Every 1–3 years as recommended
- **Bloodwork**: Annually for seniors; every 2–3 years for healthy adults
