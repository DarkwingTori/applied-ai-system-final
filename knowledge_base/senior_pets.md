# Senior Pet Care Guidelines

## When Is My Pet a Senior?

- **Small dogs** (under 20 lbs): Senior at 10–12 years
- **Medium dogs** (20–50 lbs): Senior at 8–10 years
- **Large dogs** (50–90 lbs): Senior at 7–8 years
- **Giant dogs** (over 90 lbs): Senior at 5–6 years
- **Cats**: Generally considered senior at 10–11 years; "super senior" at 15+

## Veterinary Care
Senior pets should have checkups every 6 months rather than annually. Blood panels, urinalysis, and blood pressure monitoring can detect age-related conditions (kidney disease, hyperthyroidism in cats, diabetes, Cushing's disease in dogs) early enough for effective management.

## Exercise Adjustments
- Maintain daily exercise but reduce intensity and duration.
- Shorter, more frequent walks are better than one long walk for arthritic pets.
- Warm up slowly; avoid sudden bursts of activity.
- Swim therapy (hydrotherapy) is excellent for arthritic dogs — low-impact and joint-friendly.
- Monitor for signs of pain: limping, difficulty rising, reluctance to climb stairs or jump.

## Nutrition Changes
- Many senior pets benefit from foods formulated for their life stage with adjusted protein, phosphorus, and antioxidant levels.
- Kidney-supportive diets (low phosphorus) may be recommended for pets with early kidney disease.
- Joint supplements (glucosamine, chondroitin, omega-3 fatty acids) may reduce arthritis symptoms.
- Monitor weight carefully; both obesity and unintended weight loss are common problems in seniors.

## Cognitive Decline (Canine/Feline Cognitive Dysfunction)
Similar to dementia in humans, cognitive dysfunction syndrome (CDS) affects many pets over age 11. Signs include:
- Disorientation or getting "stuck" in corners
- Changes in sleep/wake cycles (wandering at night)
- Forgetting house training
- Reduced interaction with family
- Staring blankly at walls

Management includes environmental enrichment, mental stimulation, prescription diets with antioxidants, and medications (selegiline for dogs). Maintaining routine is critical for cognitively impaired pets.

## Mobility and Comfort
- Provide orthopedic or memory foam beds to cushion arthritic joints.
- Add ramps or steps to help pets reach couches, beds, or car seats without jumping.
- Place food and water bowls at a comfortable height to reduce neck strain.
- Ensure litter boxes have low entry points for arthritic cats.
- Non-slip rugs on hard floors prevent falls in dogs with joint issues or muscle weakness.

## Dental Health
Dental disease is more advanced in senior pets and can contribute to heart, kidney, and liver problems. Regular dental cleanings under anesthesia (with pre-anesthetic bloodwork) are safe for most healthy seniors and can significantly improve quality of life.

## End-of-Life Considerations
Quality of life scales help owners assess whether a senior pet is experiencing more good days than bad. Talk to your vet about pain management options, palliative care, and — when the time comes — humane euthanasia to prevent suffering.
