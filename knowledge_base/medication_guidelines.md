# Pet Medication Guidelines

## General Principles
Never give your pet human medications without explicit veterinary guidance. Many common human drugs (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, certain antihistamines) are toxic to pets. Always complete the full course of prescribed medications even if your pet seems better.

## Timing and Consistency
Most pet medications work best when given at consistent times each day. This is especially important for:
- Antibiotics: Consistent timing maintains therapeutic blood levels and reduces antibiotic resistance.
- Seizure medications (phenobarbital, potassium bromide): Must never be missed; missed doses can trigger breakthrough seizures.
- Thyroid medications: Irregular dosing causes fluctuating hormone levels.
- Insulin: Must be given at the same time as meals; never give insulin without feeding first.

## Administering Medications

### Pills and Tablets
- Hide in a small amount of soft food (peanut butter, cream cheese, pill pockets) for most dogs.
- Cats are harder; try "pilling" by gently opening the mouth, placing the pill as far back as possible, and stroking the throat.
- Never crush time-release tablets; check with your vet before crushing any pill.
- Pill guns/pill poppers can make administration easier for resistant pets.

### Liquid Medications
- Use a syringe for accurate dosing; avoid eyeballing measurements.
- For oral liquids, insert the syringe into the side of the mouth (cheek pouch) rather than over the tongue to prevent gagging.
- Refrigerate if instructed; shake if directed.

### Topical Medications (flea/tick, ear, eye drops)
- Apply spot-on flea treatments between shoulder blades so the pet cannot lick them.
- Hold eye drops or ear treatments in place for 30–60 seconds for absorption.
- Wash hands after applying any topical medication.

## Missing a Dose
- If it is close to the time of the next dose, skip the missed dose.
- Never double dose.
- For critical medications (insulin, anti-seizure drugs), contact your vet immediately if a dose is missed.

## Side Effects to Watch For
- Vomiting or diarrhea (especially with antibiotics or NSAIDs)
- Lethargy or unusual tiredness
- Facial swelling, hives, or difficulty breathing (allergic reaction — emergency)
- Loss of appetite persisting more than 24 hours
- Changes in drinking or urination frequency

## Storing Medications
- Store in a cool, dry place unless refrigeration is required.
- Keep all medications out of reach of pets and children.
- Dispose of expired medications through a pharmacy take-back program; do not flush.

## Flea and Tick Prevention
Monthly topical or oral flea/tick preventatives should be given consistently, ideally on the same date each month. Year-round prevention is recommended in most U.S. climates as fleas and ticks can be active even in cooler months.
